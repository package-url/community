## Notes

- Meeting minutes are being kept and will be published, and the meeting will be recorded with Google Meet video and Gemini "note-taking".

- Introductions.
- Summary
  - The group designated the ECMA association as the change controller for the Package-URL specification.
  - This structure aims to finalize the Second Edition by tightening conformance standards.
  - Discussions confirmed that the new git PURL type is ready for registration.
  - The group discussed the need for an outreach campaign to key open source projects and the need for a mailing list in addition to current channels.
  - Discussions explored adding private identification capabilities to distinguish internal components from public open source packages. Members debated whether to use visibility flags or specific new types for these identifiers.
  - The group analyzed the limitations of hash-based indexing versus human-operable identifiers, agreeing that both precision levels are necessary for robust security analysis.
- **Meeting Opening and Introductions**: Philippe Ombredanne opened the meeting, noting that the call was being recorded solely for transcription purposes and not for public distribution. The team performed a roll call and reviewed the agenda, which included new discussion items from Van Lindberg, a Git type update from Jon Moroney, and a VERS specification update from Jonatan Männchen. J.R. Hill (also known as Justin) introduced themself as an open source enthusiast currently working at Shopify with a background in supply chain security at Amazon.
- **Philippe Ombredanne's Event Updates**: Philippe Ombredanne provided an update on two recent one-day events in Brussels: the Open Source Policy Ecosystem Forum and the European Open Source Security Forum, both organized by the Linux Foundation. These events featured approximately 40 to 50 participants from organizations such as IBM, SAP, Red Hat, and the FreeBSD project. A key takeaway from the discussions was a consensus that the current vulnerability disclosure and advisory ecosystem is disorganized.
- **ISO/ECMA Update**: Michael Herzog reported progress on the ISO front, noting that while the ECMA process is bureaucratic, they received communication indicating a target timeline of approximately six months for approval rather than 11 months. Michael Herzog clarified that for the purposes of these standards, "International Standard" specifically refers to ISO or IEC, and the team must be precise with terminology when writing specifications.
- **IANA Registration and Security Considerations**: Michael Herzog updated the group on the IANA registration for the PURL (Package-URL) specification. The team secured an agreement that ECMA will serve as the change controller, with Philippe Ombredanne remaining the contact. Regarding security, the requirement for a formal threat model document was removed, provided that the PURL specification itself incorporates security considerations. Michael Herzog plans to include these security considerations in the next edition of ECMA-427.
- **PURL Second Edition Focus**: Michael Herzog outlined the strategy for the ECMA-427 PURL Second Edition, which will focus on tightening the specification and prioritizing conformance. The plan involves removing non-normative normalization discussions from the specification and redoing the test suite to simplify the structure, distinguishing clearly between canonical and non-canonical forms. Michael Herzog intends to move background material and tool-specific guidance into non-normative documentation to ensure the specification remains focused on defining valid canonical PURLs.
- **Outreach to Open Source Projects**: Philippe Ombredanne proposed an outreach campaign to encourage key open source projects that have adopted PURLs to ensure their compliance with the specification. Specifically, Philippe Ombredanne mentioned discussions with the CIO of Anchore, the maintainers of Grype and Syft, regarding the need for correctly formatted PURLs in their vulnerability programs.
- **Fedora PURL Adoption**: Jon Moroney reported that Fedora is considering adopting PURLs for their entire build system. This development highlights the importance of the team's ongoing efforts to integrate support for Software ID and resolve packaging challenges. Fedora developers face unresolved questions regarding how to represent packages that include patches or vendored dependencies, such as those found in RPM spec files.
- **Communication Infrastructure**: The group discussed the need for an outbound communication strategy, such as a mailing list, to keep the community informed without fragmenting discussions into platforms outside of GitHub or Slack. Michael Herzog evaluated options including Listmonk, Groups.io, and Google Groups, noting that while Groups.io has network effects, the team is cautious about costs and administrative overhead. A decision is expected within the week.
- **Git Type and Testing**: Jon Moroney requested an update on the progress of the Git PURL type, questioning if the existing tests are sufficient. Michael Herzog suggested not focusing on fixing the current test suite, as the team plans to implement a new, simpler test structure. The current suite suffers from a lack of failing tests, making it difficult to distinguish between correct and incorrect implementations.
- **PURL Type Registration Challenges**: Michael Herzog and Jon Moroney discussed the difficulty of validating PURLs that are syntactically correct but lack a registered PURL type. They agreed that it is unreasonable to hard-fail these PURLs, but there is a need for a clearer process for identifying community authority on new type proposals, particularly for amorphous projects like Git.
- **Implementer Working Group**: Michael Herzog proposed creating a working group specifically for PURL implementers—both commercial and open source—to facilitate communication regarding changes to testing and specifications. This group would aim to capture a broader audience than just package manager maintainers, though Jonatan Männchen noted that they could bring specific communications into existing OpenSSF meetings where core maintainers already congregate.
- **Git Type Pull Request and Software ID**: Philippe Ombredanne confirmed that the Git PURL type pull request is ready to merge following a final review of comments from J.R. Hill. The team also discussed the ongoing commitment to finalize the Software ID, which has support from the OpenSSF and the Linux kernel project.
- **Apache Software Foundation Update**: Philippe Ombredanne noted that the Apache Software Foundation is struggling with the complexity of software distribution and tracking. The foundation is working on a "Apache Trusted Release" initiative to map project versions and download locations, which is a significant challenge due to the variety of release methods used across Apache projects.
- **VERS Specification Update**: Jonatan Männchen updated the team on the VERS specification, stating that they are working on a second version following feedback from a mathematician that identified inconsistencies in the fork semantics of the initial proposal. The team aims to simplify the proposal by using existing primitives, with a more complete version expected in two weeks.
- **Private PURL Proposal**: Van Lindberg presented a proposal for a "pkg:private" PURL type to address the difficulty of identifying internal, non-published code within an organization. Van argued that for internal code, the location is often the identity of the code, and a PURL could act as a map to locate internal components during vulnerability analysis, such as when tracking dependencies like log4j.
- **Managing Internal Code and Monorepos**: The discussion highlighted that internal code is often managed differently than public open source code, with monorepo structures presenting unique challenges. Van Lindberg explained that the proposal includes "repository internal path" features to help distinguish code modules within large monorepos. Michael Herzog noted that while this is a valid use case for internal consistency, it is important to distinguish this from the team's broader goal of supporting public PURLs.
- **Namespace and Type Strategy**: Jon Moroney questioned whether the "private" requirement should be its own PURL type or an attribute of existing types, noting that the namespace constraint is often provided by the repository URL. Van Lindberg clarified that the proposal is intended to allow for a semi-deterministic creation of PURLs where two different people within an organization would generate the same PURL for internal code.
- **Private PURL as an Override**: Michael Herzog interpreted the proposal as an override mechanism that allows organizations to clearly label sensitive or private internal code within a Bill of Materials, preventing the inadvertent publication of private information. Van Lindberg confirmed this is a designed use case.
- **Internal Build System Examples**: J.R. Hill shared insights from their time at Amazon, describing "Brazil" as an internal build system that creates unique build artifacts. J.R. Hill noted that in such systems, components often cascade into each other, and simply identifying a version is insufficient for understanding vulnerability exposure. J.R. Hill suggested that having a specific model for these internal artifacts could be beneficial for security teams.
- **Visibility Flags vs. Custom Types**: The group debated whether to implement visibility flags (e.g., public vs. private) or use custom "x-" prefixed types to accommodate organization-specific needs. Philippe Ombredanne pointed out that existing package manifests like Composer and npm use a "private" attribute, which suggests a possible precedent. The group did not reach a consensus, with some members cautioning against deviating too far from the core purpose of PURLs.
- **Location as Identity**: Van Lindberg discussed a design feature where the location serves as the identity of the code within an organization. Philippe Ombredanne explained that package managers, such as those used for Python or npm, function with default locations, and while these can be made explicit, the functional difference is minimal within the specification.
- **Service Native ID Proposal**: Van Lindberg proposed adding a "native ID" to the service specification to act as an "escape hatch" for increased specificity. This ID would allow users to reference deployment-specific details—such as an Amazon Resource Name (ARN) for AWS or specific identifiers for Azure—without embedding excessive information directly into the primary locator. Philippe Ombredanne expressed minimal concern regarding this addition, as the topic was previously discussed.
- **Registry Requirement for Services**: Philippe Ombredanne questioned whether a registry is necessary for the proposed service identifier. Van Lindberg argued against a registry, stating that because services are network-accessible and have a controlling authority via their domain, a registry is not required. Philippe Ombredanne acknowledged this rationale but expressed concern regarding the high level of complexity inherent in the consoles of major providers like Google, Amazon, and Azure, emphasizing the need for a practical solution.
- **Concerns Regarding Custom Naming Schemes**: Jon Moroney raised a concern about organizations developing individual naming schemes for software, noting that such a practice is antithetical to the goal of establishing common names for software.
- **SBOM Forum Status**: Michael Herzog mentions that activity on the SBOM forum has been quiet and stated that they need to contact Tom Alrich to check on the progress of the working group.
- **OmniBOR and Indexing Approaches**: Jon Moroney introduced OmniBOR as an alternative indexing modality compared to PURL. While Jon suggested it is a different way of indexing, Van Lindberg noted that OmniBOR is specifically tied to compilation processes. Michael Herzog reflected on a previous US government bid meeting regarding CycloneDX, noting that while OmniBOR is an interesting component type, it is effectively a hash-based approach.
- **Comparison of PURL and Hash-based Identifiers**: Philippe Ombredanne characterized PURL as a "street address" and software identifiers like OmniBOR as "GPS coordinates," arguing that both levels of precision are required. The group discussed the limitations of relying solely on hashes for security, with Philippe Ombredanne referencing a recent GitHub action compromise at Red Hat where signed artifacts were still vulnerable to malware, demonstrating that hashes provide only a false sense of security. Philippe Ombredanne added that they are collaborating with Software Heritage to scan software archives, which will eventually allow for queries by both software IDs and PURL.
- **Human Operability of PURL**: Jon Moroney observed that PURL is human-operable, allowing users to intuit the meaning of a package in a way that is not possible with raw hashes. Philippe Ombredanne and the other participants agreed that hashes are neither expressive nor intuitive for human users.
- **Closing Remarks**: Van Lindberg expressed appreciation for the opportunity to share these ideas and receive feedback from the group. The participants concluded the meeting with final farewells.
- The meeting was adjourned.
